Smallest

Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system - only slightly larger than the Earth's moon.

If the sun were as tall as a typical front door, Earth would be the size of a nickel and Mercury would be about as big as a green pea.

Insider

It is the closest planet to the sun at a distance of about 58 million km (36 million miles) or 0.39 AU.

Long Days, Short Years

One day on Mercury (the time it takes for Mercury to rotate or spin once with respect to the stars) takes 59 Earth days.

One day-night cycle on Mercury takes 175.97 Earth days.

Mercury makes a complete orbit around the sun (a year in Mercury time) in just 88 Earth days.

Rough Surface

Mercury is a rocky planet, also known as a terrestrial planet. Mercury has a solid, cratered surface, much like the Earth's moon.

Can't Breathe It

Mercury's thin atmosphere, or exosphere, is composed mostly of oxygen (O2), sodium (Na), hydrogen (H2), helium (He), and potassium (K).

Atoms that are blasted off the surface by the solar wind and micrometeoroid impacts create Mercury's exosphere.

Moonless & Ringless

There are no rings around Mercury and it has no moons.

Tough Place to Live

No evidence for life has been found on Mercury. Daytime Temperatures can reach 430 degrees Celsius (800 degrees Fahrenheit) and drop to -180 degrees Celsius (-290 degrees Fahrenheit) at night. It is unlikely life (as we know it) could survive on this planet.

Caloris Basin

The Caloris Basin is the largest crater on Mercury.

At roughly 1,550 km (960 miles) in diameter, the Caloris Basin is one of the largest impact basins in the solar system.

Magnetic Tornadoes

Mercury has a global magnetic field.

Its magnetic field has developed leaks that can allow the flow of solar wind, which has its own magnetic field.

Occasionaly the result is a funnelling of magnetic fields into a magnetic tornado.

Magnetic tornadoes allow solar wind plasma to directly hit Mercury's surface, blasting atoms into Mercury's exosphere.

Observation

The earliest known observations of Mercury are dated from the 14th century BC!

NASA has sent two space probes to observe Mercury.

Mariner 10

In 1975, Mariner 10 gave us the first close-up view of Mars. Mariner 10 photographed about 45% of the planet.

MESSENGER

MESSENGER did much more imaging, including the first color images, during three fly by missions between 2008 and 2009.
